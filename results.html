Glioma Overview

General information about this tumor type

Gliomas are tumors that occur in the brain and spinal cord. They begin in the glial cells that surround and support nerve cells. Gliomas can be low grade (slow growing) or high grade (fast growing).

  • Weakness or numbness in a part of the body

Treatment Options Explained

Surgery

Recommended

Surgical removal of the tumor, either partially or completely, depending on its location and size.

Benefits

  • Can remove or reduce tumor size immediately
  • Provides tissue for accurate diagnosis
  • May relieve symptoms caused by pressure
  • Can be curative for some tumor types

Considerations

  • Invasive procedure with general risks of surgery
  • May not be possible if tumor is in a critical area
  • Recovery time can be significant
  • May not remove all tumor cells

Radiation Therapy

Recommended

Uses high-energy beams to kill tumor cells and shrink tumors. Can be delivered externally or internally.

Benefits

  • Non-invasive (external beam)
  • Can target specific areas
  • Effective for controlling tumor growth
  • Can be used after surgery to kill remaining cells

Considerations

  • Side effects may include fatigue, hair loss, skin problems
  • May cause damage to nearby healthy tissue
  • Usually requires multiple treatment sessions
  • Long-term side effects possible

Chemotherapy

Recommended

Uses drugs to kill rapidly dividing cells, including tumor cells. Can be taken orally or intravenously.

Benefits

  • Can reach tumor cells throughout the body
  • Effective for certain tumor types
  • Can be combined with other treatments
  • Options for different delivery methods

Considerations

  • Side effects may include nausea, fatigue, hair loss
  • May affect healthy rapidly dividing cells
  • Blood-brain barrier may limit effectiveness
  • Requires careful monitoring
